# Session 01 — The Resting Wheel

**Date In-World**: Early autumn, Year 50 of the Peace  
**Zyx's Portent Dice**: 3 and 17 (reset at next dawn)

---

## Scene 1 — The Resting Wheel, Briarfen (Common Room, Evening)

The Resting Wheel is one of those inns that has been well-maintained for so long it has become almost historical. The fire in the hearth is the good kind — steady and orange, not flashy about it. Half a dozen other patrons occupy the room at various distances: a merchant's assistant going over columns of numbers, two farmhands with their boots off, an old man in the far corner who has been nursing the same cup for an hour.

The party has just returned from their meeting with Aldous Finch, the Regional Lord's steward, who received them in a small rented office two streets over with the professional warmth of a man performing courtesy toward people he didn't expect to see again. He provided: the quest, a contract written in tight legalistic script before he'd ever met them, and twelve gold pieces each as advance payment. He also provided a single sheet of directions to Mosswick, attached to a map so optimistically inaccurate it suggests the cartographer had never left Briarfen.

They have their coin. They have their gear. They have the morning, after which they should head north through the Ashwood. What to do with the evening is, as yet, undecided.

The old man in the far corner is watching them over the rim of his cup. He has the look of someone who was someone, once — not threatening, just attentive in a way that seems practiced.

One block east, if anyone knew to look, is the Stoneback Brewery.

**Midge:** She pulls out Volume 48 and writes — *The contract was signed with a flourish that Aldous Finch clearly found excessive but could not argue with — Midge's signature alone worth the weight of the gold in presence and panache* — then caps her pen, looks up, and her eyes go straight to the old man. He's watching. Has been watching. Not curious-watching. *Knows-something* watching. She sketches his face quickly in the Chronicles — jaw, eyes, shoulders — then leans toward Grunka, keeping her voice low and bright like she's saying something cheerful: *"Don't look immediately, but the old man in the corner has been watching us since we got back. Not curious-watching. Knows-something watching."* She taps the sketch twice. *"I'm going to get us both drinks and take the long way round."* She stands, smooths her armor, and moves toward the bar in a careful arc that passes near his table — close enough to take in his hands, his boots, what's on the table besides the cup, whether he's got the look of a local.

**Reginald:** He observes Midge's theatrical arc with the resigned recognition of a man who has seen this before. She is not wrong about the old man — she is, however, making something conspicuous that didn't need to be. He sets down his cup, straightens his posture, and walks directly to the old man's table. No arc. No subterfuge. He rests a hand on the back of the chair across: *"Good evening, sir. I am Sir Reginald Pompsworth the Third, formerly of the Order of the Vigilant Flame, currently engaged in service to Regional Lord Harwick's steward in the matter of Mosswick village. You appear to be watching our party with some purpose. I find it generally more efficient, and considerably more ethical, to address such things directly rather than at an angle. May I ask your name, and whether there is something we might do for one another?"*

**Grunka:** She watches Reginald walk a straight line across the room and feels the particular warmth she always does when he does exactly that. No pretense. Good man. She picks up her cup, crosses, pulls out the chair beside Reginald's — not hovering, not crowding, just settling in like this was already decided. She sets her cup down and looks at the old man properly. The watching-eyes. The posture of someone carrying something they've been carrying for a while. *"I'm Grunka,"* she says, because he'll want to know who all of them are. Then she looks at him the way she looks at ground that feels wrong — carefully, without flinching. *"You watched us leave after the Finch meeting. You're still here. That's not idle curiosity."* She doesn't ask him to talk. She just waits. Lets the space sit. After a breath: *"Would you like some tea?"*

**Zyx:** The pipe goes out. He notices — the ember dying just as the old man's attention settles on the table across the room — and takes a moment to relight it with a small flame on his fingertip. Old habit. He doesn't move. Grunka offered tea. Right instinct. Reginald introduced himself at full length, as Reginald does. The small one is at the bar pretending to be invisible, which means she's paying more attention than anyone. Good. Let them work. He studies the old man's hands from across the room. The way someone holds a cup tells you a great deal about what kind of weight they're used to carrying. He catches Grunka's eye and gives the smallest nod. *You're doing fine. Keep going.*

The old man watches Reginald's approach with the expression of someone who has been waiting for a thing and isn't entirely sure he wanted it to arrive. When Reginald finishes his introduction — complete with order name, lord's title, and the word "ethical" — the old man's mouth does something complicated that isn't quite a smile.

He looks at Grunka. At his empty cup. Then back at Grunka.

"...Yes," he says. "Actually."

He sets the cup down. Folds his hands on the table. Plain traveler's clothes, carefully mended — the mending of someone who keeps things long past when a less careful person would have replaced them. His hands are scarred in the pattern of a life spent in close work with bladed things.

"My name is Edric. Edric Holt. I was with the Monster Hunters' Guild for thirty-one years before they closed the doors and turned the place into a display of antique crossbows." He pauses. "I came down from the north three weeks ago. I've been sitting here because I didn't know what to do with what I saw up there, and there was nobody I trusted to tell."

He looks at the table for a moment.

"You're going to Mosswick." Not a question.

"I thought about stopping whoever they hired to go. Telling them it wasn't worth the trouble." He looks at Grunka directly. Then at Reginald. Then toward the bar where Midge is doing her best impression of someone who definitely isn't eavesdropping. "But all of you. You've got that look. The Guild used to call it readiness. I haven't seen it in twenty years."

He unfolds his hands. On the table beneath them — kept out of sight until now — is a small folded piece of paper. He doesn't push it across yet. Just lets it become visible.

"The wolves in the Ashwood aren't rabid. They're not going to attack you. They're..." He stops. Considers. "Confused. Like they're hearing something from underground that they don't have words for. I've seen it before. Not for fifty years. But I've seen it." He exhales. "The Void King is a bedtime story these days. I know that. But I kept the old Guild records. And the records say: when the animals go wrong, something is waking up that shouldn't be."

He looks across the room toward Zyx's armchair by the fire. Takes in the ancient robes, the pipe, the complete lack of urgency.

His voice, when it comes again, is quieter.

"...Is that who I think it is?"

*(Round 2)*

**Zyx:** The smoke curls. He doesn't look over immediately. *"Tell him yes,"* he says to Reginald, in the tone of a man asking someone to pass the salt. *"It saves time."* Then he raises his pipe across the room in acknowledgment. *"Yes, yes. Sit down, Mr. Holt. Or come over here, whichever. The paper. Show me the paper."* He settles back into the armchair. The portent dice sit in the back of his mind — 3 and 17. The pattern is tightening.

**Grunka:** The wrongness in the ground this morning had felt like an old scar being pressed. She'd said nothing because she was watching to see if the party would flinch or lean in. Edric Holt leaned in. She pushes the cup of tea a half-inch closer to him and says, plainly: *"I felt it too. This afternoon, riding out of Briarfen. The ground is wrong in the same way it was wrong twenty years ago, up in the Krethian hills, before whatever came out of the ruins and killed my people. Fourteen of fifteen. I have six journals about it. I have been looking for someone who kept records for twenty years."* A pause. *"Bring your chair over, Mr. Holt. And bring the paper. How are you feeling about all of this? Truly."*

**Reginald:** The weight of it settles across his shoulders like a second set of armor. He sets down his tankard — carefully, precisely in the center of the ring it had already made — and straightens. *"Mr. Holt,"* he says, and his voice comes out steadier than he feels, *"I am Sir Reginald Pompsworth the Third, formerly of the Order of the Vigilant Flame, and I want you to understand that everything you have just shared will be treated with the full gravity it deserves."* He opens his Ethics Journal to a fresh page — the reflex of a man trained to report upward, even when there is no longer anyone above him to report to. He looks at Grunka. *"Fourteen of fifteen. I am sorry. That is a loss I have no procedure for, and I will not pretend otherwise."* Then back to Holt: *"Please. Sit with us. Show us the paper. We are listening."*

**Midge:** She slides off her barstool — decisive thump, mostly a quiet halfling landing — and drifts toward the table as if she had been planning this all along and was not in fact standing at the bar with her mouth open. She drops into the chair with the best sightline to the paper and flips open the Chronicles to a fresh page. She writes *VOLUME 48, CHAPTER 1* in careful letters and underlines it twice. *"Midge Thistlewick,"* she tells Edric, since nobody introduced her. *"I'm with them. Have been from the start. The Chronicles will need the full account — that's my ongoing record of these events, for posterity — so when you show us the paper, if you could speak clearly and maybe not too fast, that would be."* She clears her throat. *"Professional courtesy."* She glances at Zyx sideways, quickly, then back at Edric like she didn't.

---

Edric Holt looks around the table. At Reginald with his fresh page and his open face. At Grunka with her hands wrapped around a cup and twenty years of patience behind her eyes. At Midge with her pen and her underlined chapter heading. At Zyx across the room, already waiting.

He picks up his chair and carries it over to Zyx's armchair. Sits across from the wizard. Sets his cup down. Unfolds the paper.

It is a travel journal's last page — his own handwriting, cramped and precise, the way people write when they're not sure they'll have time to write more. Five entries, dated over three weeks of travel down from the north:

> *Day 1 north of Millhaven: wolves at Old Millhouse ruin again. Third time this week. Not hunting. Sitting. Listening to something.*
> *Day 3: silence in the Ashwood for a full two miles around the ruin. No birdsong. Smell like cold stone in summer. Have not smelled that in fifty years.*
> *Day 5: met two travelers on the Mosswick road going south. Said they'd heard sounds from underground near the old grain tower at night. Low and rhythmic. Turned back.*
> *Day 7: checked Old Millhouse personally. The stones are warm. In autumn. The Guild records call this a Stage Two Emergence indicator. We have not had a Stage Two in fifty years.*
> *Day 9: heading for Briarfen. Don't know who to tell.*

He sets the paper in the center of the table.

"Old Millhouse is half a day into the Ashwood from here," he says. "It's a ruin — nobody's been there in years. It's on your road to Mosswick." He looks at Zyx. "Stage Two Emergence. That's what the records call it. Means the influence has begun pushing through into the physical world. Not fully. But enough to warm the stones." He's quiet for a moment. "In the Guild records, from fifty years ago, Stage Two preceded the first disappeared villages by four days."

The table is very quiet.

Then Edric says: "There's one more thing." He looks at his cup. "One block east of here. The Stoneback Brewery." He says the name like it costs him something. "Heldra Stoneback. She was there for the sealing, fifty years ago. She knows more than any of us." He finally looks at Zyx directly. "I've been working up the nerve to go see her for three weeks and haven't managed it. She has no use for old Guild men who weren't there." A pause. "But she might talk to you."

*(Zyx — Heldra decision)*

**Zyx:** The silence sits for a moment longer than it needs to. He turns his cup in his hands and does not look at the door. *"Heldra."* The name comes out flat. Not surprised. Just... named. He sets the cup down. *"Yes. She would know. She was the one who drove the anchor pin on the northern face. I was forty meters back calling the containment. We did not speak much afterward."* He pushes himself up from the chair with the unhurried slowness of someone who has learned that urgency is usually a feeling, not a fact. *"I have been aware she was one block east for some time."* He picks up his staff. *"There are conversations one defers. I have deferred this one. It would be unkind to let her go on not knowing. Given what the stones are doing."* He moves toward the door at the speed he moves everywhere — not slow because he is old, but slow because he stopped seeing the point of haste sometime in the third century. *"You may come. She dislikes guild men, not people. Try to be a person."*

The party follows him out into the autumn evening. One block east.

## Scene 2 — Stoneback Brewery, Briarfen (Taproom, Evening)

The Stoneback Brewery occupies a building that was purpose-built for the work and has been run with the same blunt efficiency ever since. No decorative sign — just a carved stone badger above the door and the smell of malt and copper that has saturated the surrounding block for eighty years. The taproom is small, serves only Stoneback ales, and closes when Heldra wants it to close.

It's near closing. Three locals nurse their final pints. Behind the bar, managing the last of the evening with the economy of motion of someone who has done this ten thousand times, is a dwarf woman of one hundred and twelve years. She is broad-shouldered and grey-haired and her face has the particular weathered quality of someone who has been paying attention for a very long time. Her forearms, resting on the bar, carry the faded marks of old burn scarring.

She looks up when the door opens.

Her eyes go to Zyx first. They stay there for exactly two seconds — long enough to confirm something she had already calculated — and then she turns back to the glass she is polishing.

"Taproom's closing," she says.

She doesn't tell them to leave.

**Grunka:** She pulls out a chair at the nearest table and sits, unhurried, hands open on the table. *"We're not here for a pint. We're here because there's something coming out of the Ashwood, and we're told you've seen it before."* She watches Heldra's hands on the glass. *"And because Zyx says you drove the anchor pin on the northern face, which means you know something the rest of us need to know."* A pause. *"How are you feeling about all of this? His showing up at your bar tonight, I mean. You've had time to prepare for it, and you still said 'taproom's closing' instead of his name."* She looks toward the back. *"I'll make tea, if you point me to the kitchen."*

**Reginald:** He removes his helm and tucks it under his arm — rude to conduct serious business with your face hidden. He stays near the door rather than presuming to sit. *"Madam Stoneback. My name is Sir Reginald Pompsworth the Third, formerly of the Order of the Vigilant Flame. The Order maintained vigil at the northern anchor site for sixty-three years following the sealing. We dissolved eleven years ago."* A pause. *"I want you to know that what you and the Brightblade Company did is not forgotten. I have the records. I have read them many times. I understand if this evening is not what you would have chosen."* He clears his throat. *"The offer of tea was genuinely thoughtful. I think you should consider it."*

**Midge:** The journal is out before she realizes it — the good leather one with the brass clasp, the one that looks like what a hero's chronicler would carry. Her hand writes automatically while she stares at the burn scars on Heldra's forearms. She's read the accounts. There's a passage. She reminds herself very firmly that she is not going to touch them. *"Heldra Stoneback — I'm Midge Thistlewick. I've been writing the Chronicles — of this, of what we're doing — and I've read everything about the original sealing, every account, and I have opinions about the Corrath account specifically, where he attributes the anchor work to General Falthen—"* She stops. Closes the journal. *"I'm sorry. We need your help. And I think maybe you've been waiting a long time for someone to come and ask. Or dreading it. I'm not sure which, and I thought you should know someone noticed the difference."*

**Zyx:** He moves to the nearest chair — the one at an angle, not directly across — and lowers himself into it with the care of someone whose joints remember everything. He doesn't look at her immediately. He looks at the table. *"The records are wrong about the anchor work. Falthen was twenty meters away when the pin went in. He had been useful earlier in the evening. He wrote it that way because he was embarrassed. I never corrected the record."* Now he looks at her. *"You said 'taproom's closing.' I noticed. I have been one block east for six days. I was aware. I kept finding reasons."* His hands rest flat on the table. *"Heldra. I came because the Ashwood is doing the thing it does before. We all know what the thing is. I also came because there are two people who are not here, and someone ought to say their names before we talk about what comes next, and I thought it should be me."* He waits.

---

The three locals in the corner have been watching this scene with increasing discomfort. Heldra jerks her head at them. They leave — quietly, efficiently, without needing to be asked twice.

She sets down the glass.

"Aldric," she says. "And Senna."

Just the two names. Said flat and fast, the way you say something you've been getting through for fifty years.

She comes around the bar. She doesn't sit at Grunka's table. She sits at the one where Zyx is. She brings two cups and a bottle of something that is not the house ale. She pours. Pushes the bottle toward Zyx.

"I know why you're here," she says. "You're here about the box."

A pause.

"There is — was — a box under the brewery floor. In the foundation, behind the cold room. I put it there the year I opened the place. Eighty years ago." Her hands are around her cup. "Three months ago it started getting warm. I've been going down every morning to check it." She doesn't drink. "Two weeks ago, I went down and the box was open. The shard was gone."

She looks at the cup. "Someone came in the night. I don't know how they got past the wards. I am a brewer, not a mage. But those wards were not simple — Brother Tolman helped me set them, the year before he died. They were not the kind of thing you walk past accidentally."

She finally drinks.

"I didn't report it because there is nobody to report it to. The Guild is a museum. The Orders are community centers. The only person who might have understood what it meant is sitting at my table having avoided coming to see me for six days, which, for the record, I also noticed."

She looks at the rest of the party — really looks, one at a time.

"Mosswick's been quiet two weeks. I know. Same night the shard went, I went up on the roof and looked north and the air was wrong." Her jaw tightens briefly, then releases. "I have been brewing ale for eighty years waiting to be wrong about knowing what wrong air feels like. I have never once been wrong."

Her eyes settle on Zyx. Quiet and direct.

"How bad is it?"

**Zyx:** He sets down his cup. Not because he is uncertain — because once it is said, it cannot be unsaid, and he has learned to honor that weight. *"Bad. And I owe you — I owe all of you — more than that word."* He looks around the table at Reginald, Midge, Grunka. They deserve to hear it properly. *"The Void King was sealed, not destroyed. There is a difference, and the difference matters now. The sealing required six objects — Void Shards, fragments of his power, willingly bound into the ritual by the Brightblade Company. One for each member. Two were destroyed with Aldric and Senna when they died. The other four were dispersed. Hidden. I helped hide them."* A pause. *"I thought we had done a sufficient job. Three months ago, I went to check on a shard I had secured myself. It was gone. No warning. No signs of force. Just gone. I came to Briarfen because my monitoring wards on the old sites began failing in a pattern. The pattern centers under Greyveil."* He lets that settle for exactly one breath. *"Someone is collecting the shards. I do not know who. I do not know why. But if the shards are brought together in the wrong way, by the wrong hands, for the wrong purpose — the seal does not strengthen. It breaks. Your shard. My shard. Two accounted for. That leaves two more, somewhere, and someone who is ahead of us."*

---

The room is very quiet.

Reginald opens his Ethics Journal, then closes it again without writing anything. He will have things to say about this later — many things, carefully reasoned — but not right now.

Midge has stopped pretending to take notes. The pen is in her hand, not moving. She is looking at the table.

Grunka says nothing. She already knew it was bad. She has known it was bad for twenty years. Knowing the precise shape of it is not a comfort, exactly, but it is something.

Heldra looks at the bottle between her and Zyx. She refills both cups.

"I was afraid of exactly that," she says. "When the box went warm in the spring, I told myself it was the foundation settling. Eighty years, stone does strange things." She exhales. "I knew it wasn't the foundation."

She drinks.

"The cold room is still there. You'll want to see where the box was — if there's anything left in the foundation stones that your wards can read." She says this to Zyx, but she glances at Grunka when she says it. Something in the way Grunka sat down and waited — something in the twenty years behind Grunka's eyes — Heldra recognizes it. A person who has been carrying knowledge alone for a long time. "In the morning, before you leave."

She stands. The meeting is over. Not because she's throwing them out — because there is nothing more to say tonight that will help, and she is 112 years old and has spent the last two weeks not sleeping.

"Mosswick first," she says. "Whatever's there. Go look at it." She picks up the bottle and puts it back behind the bar. "And whoever did this — whoever came in the night and got past Tolman's wards — they know something we don't yet. They had a reason to move now." She looks at Zyx one last time. "Find the other two shards before they do."

She goes through the back.

## Transition — Morning, Stoneback Brewery Cold Room

Before first light, Heldra brings them down.

The cold room is exactly as she described — an old foundation room behind the main cellar, used for nothing now. The box is iron, lid open. The interior is scorched in a ring where something warm sat for a very long time, then stopped. Brother Tolman's wards are intact on the outer door. They were not breached — they were simply bypassed, passed through cleanly as if they weren't there. The kind of bypass that requires either a key that knows the specific ward structure, or a measure of void magic that doesn't care about light-aligned wards at all.

Zyx spends ten minutes with Detect Magic. His findings: the residue in the box is consistent with a Void Shard — old, cold, deeply bound. But in the stone of the foundation around it, faint traces. Not identifiable. But deliberate: the approach was measured and specific. This was someone who knew exactly what they were looking for and exactly where it was. Not a thief. An agent.

He doesn't say this aloud. He says: "Yes. As expected." Heldra nods.

She gives them each a full breakfast before they leave. Reginald thanks her three times, formally. Midge steals a small decorative cask tap. Grunka asks Heldra how she's feeling about everything. Heldra says: "Ask me when you've been to Mosswick and come back." Grunka says: "Fair."

They leave Briarfen as the sun comes up.

## Scene 3 — The Ashwood (The Road to Mosswick, Half Day In)

The Ashwood is old forest — the kind that was old before Briarfen existed, before the roads were put in, before anyone named the trees. In autumn it is beautiful in a way that does not require you to enjoy it. The leaves are going gold and orange and the light comes through in long horizontal columns and the road is soft underfoot with fallen needles.

It is also, today, completely silent.

Not quiet. Silent. No birdsong. No wind-rustle of small things in the underbrush. The horses are walking but walking carefully, the way horses walk when they are deciding whether to bolt. Half a day out of Briarfen, exactly where Edric Holt's notes said they would, the road bends around a low hillock and there are the ruins of Old Millhouse — a collapsed stone structure that was probably a grain distribution point two centuries ago, now just three walls and half a floor and a lintel standing in the middle of the forest with nothing to protect.

Around the ruins: seventeen wolves.

Not a pack — seventeen wolves don't form a single pack. These are animals from different territories, different ranges, brought together by something other than hunger. They are not hunting. They are sitting and standing and pacing in a loose ring around the ruin, and some of them are looking at the ground and some of them are looking at nothing, and all of them have their ears flat in a way that is not aggression.

Confused, Edric said. Hearing something.

The stones of Old Millhouse are warm — you can feel it from ten feet away, a faint radiant heat coming off the old granite in the autumn morning. Wrong.

In the center of the collapsed floor, where the ground is exposed: a crack in the earth, narrow, running east-west for about four feet. From it: no sound. But something — a quality of air, a faint pressure at the edge of perception — coming up.

Grunka has felt this before. Twenty years ago, in the Krethian hills. She knows what this is. The wolves know what this is.

**Zyx:** He dismounts slowly, makes no sudden movements. The wolves don't concern him particularly — animals have always been better at sensing this than people credit them for. He walks toward the nearest stone, holds his hand out without touching, and feels the warmth. *"Yes,"* he says, mostly to himself. Then louder: *"This is Stage Two. The warmth is kinetic bleed — the Void King's influence passing through the material layer. The stones are not generating heat. They are receiving it."* He moves to the crack and crouches at its edge — studying the air pressure, not with a spell, just with four hundred years of listening. *"The crack can be sealed. Temporarily. I need to know if anyone feels anything — nausea, pressure behind the eyes, a sound just below hearing. Tell me honestly. The Void King's influence at this stage is not conscious, but it is not neutral. It samples. It makes impressions."* He looks back at the party. *"The Brightblade Company found three of these in the months before the emergence became visible. We collapsed two. The third we did not collapse in time. Greyveil is what happens when you do not collapse the third one."*

**Reginald:** He closes his eyes first — not for Divine Sense yet, but for honesty. He has been attributing the pressure behind his eyes to fatigue since before they reached the ruin. Zyx said *honestly*. *"Yes. Pressure behind the eyes. Something sub-auditory — I cannot say whether it is a sound or merely the shape of one. It began, I believe, before we reached the ruin."* He pauses. *"It does not feel hostile. It feels attentive. As though something has noticed that I am here and is forming an opinion."* He looks at the crack, then at Zyx. *"I am going to use Divine Sense now. I want to know what I am standing next to before I tell you whether I can be of any practical assistance. If that changes your timing or your approach, please say so immediately. I will wait for your word."* His hand rests on his sword pommel — not drawing. Just resting. An old habit. Something to hold.

**Midge:** The seventeen wolves get her first — not the crack, not the warmth, not the pressure, though yes, absolutely, thumbs pressing gently against the back of her skull. *Wolves, confused.* She has her notebook out before she consciously decides to have it. She narrates under her breath: *"Midge Thistlewick stood at the edge of the unknowable and took very good notes."* She writes: *Stage Two. Kinetic bleed. Wolves confused, not aggressive. Crack. Wrong.* Then: *Pressure. Yes. Something I'm not quite hearing.* She looks up at Zyx. *"I feel it. The pressure. And there's a — it's not a sound, it's where a sound would be if I could hear it. You said it samples. Is that what happened to them? It sampled them and they don't know what they are anymore?"* She takes three steps closer to the crack than is probably wise. Not because she isn't afraid. She is absolutely afraid. *"Tell me what to do to help seal it. I have very steady hands and I do exactly what I'm told when the situation calls for it."* A beat. *"The situation calls for it."*

**Grunka:** Twenty years. She stands at the edge of the wolf ring, watching them move wrong, and she knows this feeling — has known it for twenty years without anyone to hand it to. She walks forward and crouches near the crack. Not as close as Zyx. Close enough. *"Krethian hills. I was twenty. There was a place where the ground felt like this — warm, and the pressure here—"* She presses two fingers above her brow, between her eyes. *"—and everything got quiet but not silent. Like the world was listening. I didn't have words for it. I've been trying to find words for twenty years."* She looks at Zyx, then at Reginald, then at Midge. *"What I felt in the hills was bigger than this. This is smaller. Younger, maybe. Or earlier."* She holds their gaze. *"The one in the hills is still there. Has been there for twenty years."* She stands. *"Tell me what sealing it requires. I can hold something in place, absorb a hit, break something that needs breaking. Point me at the work."*

---

Reginald closes his eyes.

He has done this ten thousand times with nothing. Morning prayers in a dissolved order. Quiet rooms. The occasional wolf that turned out to be just a wolf. He reaches outward with Divine Sense — the paladin's gift for perceiving what ordinary senses cannot — and for the first time in fifteen years of practice, something answers.

Not a creature. Not a fiend, not the way he was trained to categorize it. More like the *shape* of presence — a held breath, something that has not yet formed but is oriented toward formation. It comes from below the crack, from deep below, from somewhere he cannot identify a distance for. It notices him noticing it.

He opens his eyes. His voice is very steady. "There is something there. It is aware of us."

Zyx, who has been watching him without appearing to watch him, nods once.

He reaches into his coat and produces chalk — actual chalk, worn to a nub — and marks four points around the crack in a pattern that is not quite a circle and not quite a square. He mutters something in a language nobody recognizes: not the Common tongue of spells, something older, something that sounds more like a contract than an incantation. The pressure in the air shifts. Reduces. Not gone — reduced, like a window closed against cold.

Zyx regards his work. "Three days," he says. "A week if it is slow. After that someone will need to return." He looks at the chalk marks. "I would prefer it to be someone who cares more than I do. I am going to have a great deal on my mind."

The wolves notice.

Some of them simply stand up and walk away — into the trees, without looking back, the way you leave a room when you're no longer sure why you came. Others stay, sitting in the leaf litter with their ears still flat, confused in a different way now: uncertain where the thing they came to attend to went. They are not dangerous. They are simply lost.

Grunka looks north. The trees thin a mile or so up the road where the Ashwood begins to give way to open farmland — and beyond that, somewhere, Mosswick. An hour away. And beyond Mosswick, three days through country that gets rougher: the Krethian hills. Whatever she felt in those hills twenty years ago, she has just told the rest of them about it for the first time. They heard her.

Midge is writing, her pen moving quickly, already composing the chapter in her head. She will get the chapter title right on the third try: *The Third Time.*

Reginald has his Ethics Journal open. He is writing nothing. He is staring at the page.

He is thinking about what it means that the thing beneath the crack noticed him back.

They leave as the sun is still climbing. Mosswick is ahead. The Ashwood remains silent around them, but the pressure has eased — or they have stopped noticing it, which is not the same thing.
